The Mathematics — Explained From First Principles

Black-Scholes Formula

C = S·N(d₁) - K·e^(-rT)·N(d₂)

P = K·e^(-rT)·N(-d₂) - S·N(-d₁)

d₁ = [ln(S/K) + (r + σ²/2)·T] / (σ·√T)

d₂ = d₁ - σ·√T
N(x) = cumulative normal distribution function (probability that a standard normal variable is less than x). Think of it as the probability of the option expiring in-the-money.

S·N(d₁) = expected value of receiving the stock IF you exercise.
K·e^(-rT)·N(d₂) = present value of paying strike K, weighted by probability of exercise.

e^(-rT) = discounting: ₹1 received in T years is worth less today. With r=6.5% and T=30 days, e^(-0.065×30/365) ≈ 0.9947.

Put-Call Parity

C - P = S - K·e^(-rT)
This is a no-arbitrage relationship. If it breaks, you can make risk-free profit. It means: knowing the call price and current stock price, you can always calculate the fair put price — they are linked. This is one of the most important concepts in all of derivatives pricing.

The Greeks — What Each Measures

Δ (Delta) = N(d₁) [call]
Δ (Delta) = N(d₁) - 1 [put]

Γ (Gamma) = N'(d₁)/(S·σ·√T)

ν (Vega) = S·N'(d₁)·√T

θ (Theta) = -[S·N'(d₁)·σ/(2√T)]
- r·K·e^(-rT)·N(d₂)

ρ (Rho) = K·T·e^(-rT)·N(d₂) [call]
Delta: If stock goes up ₹1, option price changes by Δ. Call delta 0.5 means option gains ₹0.50 per ₹1 stock move.

Gamma: How fast delta changes. High gamma = delta changes rapidly with stock price. Highest for at-the-money options near expiry.

Vega: Option price change per 1% change in volatility. Important for understanding vol risk.

Theta: Daily time decay — how much the option loses per day just from time passing. Option sellers earn theta. Negative for buyers.

Rho: Sensitivity to interest rate changes. Matters more for long-dated options and in high-rate environments.

Implied Volatility — Newton-Raphson

σ_{n+1} = σ_n - (BS(σ_n) - Market_Price)
÷ Vega(σ_n)

Repeat until |BS(σ) - Price| < 0.001
We observe the market option price and ask: what volatility, when plugged into Black-Scholes, gives exactly that price? We can't solve analytically, so we use Newton-Raphson — an iterative method. Start with a guess (say σ=20%), compute the BS price, compare to market price, adjust σ, repeat. Converges in 5-10 iterations.

Why Black-Scholes Works

The model assumes the stock follows Geometric Brownian Motion — prices are lognormally distributed (can't go below zero) and returns are normally distributed. By continuously delta-hedging, you can replicate any option payoff using the stock + cash, which gives a unique fair price. The key insight is that in a complete market, there is exactly one arbitrage-free price.

Key Assumptions & Failures

BS assumes: constant volatility (violated — vol smiles exist), continuous trading (can't hedge perfectly in reality), no dividends (fixable), no jumps (crash risk exists). The vol smile — implied vol varies with strike — is the biggest failure. Real traders use BS as a quoting convention but manage the model's limitations with local vol and stochastic vol models (Heston).

Interview Insight: Vol Surface

If BS were correct, implied vol would be flat across all strikes and maturities. In reality, it forms a surface — OTM puts have higher IV than ATM (crash protection demand), and short-dated ATM have different IV than long-dated. This surface encodes the market's view of tail risk and term structure of uncertainty. Managing the vol surface is a core quant strategy skill.
